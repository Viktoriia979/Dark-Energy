Dark Energy:

Dark energy is a mysterious form of energy that appears to fill all of space. Unlike ordinary matter and dark matter, dark energy does not clump together into stars, planets, or galaxies. Scientists cannot see or directly detect dark energy, but they believe it exists because of its effects on the universe. It is estimated to make up about 68% of the total energy and matter in the universe, making it the largest known component of the cosmos.

Dark energy is needed to explain why the expansion of the universe is speeding up. For many years, scientists expected gravity to slow the expansion that began with the Big Bang. However, observations showed that distant galaxies are moving away from each other faster and faster. To explain this unexpected acceleration, scientists proposed the existence of dark energy, which seems to act as a repulsive force on the largest scales of the universe.

Scientists discovered evidence for dark energy in the late 1990s by studying distant exploding stars called supernovae. These observations showed that the universe was expanding at an accelerating rate rather than slowing down. Additional evidence comes from studies of the cosmic microwave background and the large-scale structure of galaxies. Although dark energy has never been directly observed, its effects provide strong evidence that it plays a major role in shaping the universe.
